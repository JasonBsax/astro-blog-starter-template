My biggest mistake was not learning more instruments early on in my life and instead waiting until now. Looking back, I can see how the years slipped by with the assumption that there would always be more time. Tomorrow, or next month, or next year would be the right moment to start. But the right moment never announces itself. It simply passes.
What I learned from that experience was to take charge and not to wait too long, because the longer you delay, the harder the goal becomes to achieve. This is true not just with music, but with almost any skill worth having. The mind and body absorb new things more readily when they are young and unburdened. With time, habits solidify, schedules fill up, and the mental flexibility required to learn something unfamiliar quietly deteriorates. What might have taken months at ten can take years when you're older.
But the deeper lesson isn't about age, it's about the psychology of waiting. We postpone the things that matter most because they feel optional. They carry the risk of failure alongside them; starting feels harder than continuing to imagine. We mistake dreaming about something for making progress on it.
The truth is, the cost of waiting is rarely visible in the moment. It only becomes clear in hindsight, when you finally sit down to begin and realize how much further along you could have been. Start now. Start imperfectly. Start before you feel ready. Because readiness, more often than not, is just another word for waiting.
